1. Effective Teaching

The Search for the Right Method

Language teaching has evolved through various methods, each with strengths and limitations:

Why have methods proven weak and limited?

Teaching is too complex to be reduced to a single method. Methods tend to be rigid, focusing only on "how" to teach (teacher's delivery) while ignoring diverse student needs, contextual factors, and the dynamic nature of learning. They cannot accommodate the multifaceted reality of classroom interactions.

The Shift from Teaching to Learning

Modern language education has shifted from teacher-centered instruction to learner-centered approaches:

Traditional Approach (Lock-Step) Modern Approach (Learner-Centered)
Fixed set of topics for all Learning based on students' needs
Assessment focused on content recall Assessment of understanding and application
Everyone learns same way at same time Varied teaching techniques for diverse learners
Teacher as "sage on the stage" Teacher as "guide on the side"

The Evolving Role of Teachers

From traditional methods to modern education, teachers now assume multiple roles:

Facilitator/Guide

Creates learning opportunities rather than transmitting knowledge

Planner

Designs lessons that consider diverse learning styles (VARK)

Assessor

Evaluates both progress and process, providing constructive feedback

Mentor

Supports emotional and cognitive development of learners

Factors of Effective Teaching (Mujjs & Reynolds, 2005)

  • Positive attitude
  • Pleasant classroom climate
  • High expectations
  • Lesson clarity
  • Effective time management
  • Strong lesson structuring
  • Variety of teaching methods
  • Incorporation of pupil ideas
  • Appropriate questioning

Note: Effectiveness is context-specific, depending on activity type, subject matter, pupil backgrounds, and personal characteristics.

2. Self-Monitoring in Language Teaching

Assumptions About Teacher Development

  1. Effective teaching relies on a strong knowledge base
  2. Teachers are often unaware of their own teaching practices
  3. Experience alone does not guarantee development
  4. Critical reflection fosters deeper insight and change

What is self-monitoring?

Definition (Armstrong & Firth, 1984): A systematic approach to the observation, evaluation and management of one's own behavior to achieve better understanding and control.

Self-monitoring is a method of reflective teaching that involves:

  1. Understanding your own teaching (strengths and weaknesses)
  2. Collecting objective information about classroom events
  3. Analyzing this information systematically
  4. Making informed decisions about teaching improvements

The Johari Window of Teacher Awareness

Open Self

Known to teacher and others

Blind Self

Known to others but not teacher

Secret Self

Known to teacher but not others

Hidden Self

Unknown to both

How is Self-Monitoring Carried Out?

  1. Personal reflection: Systematic thinking about teaching experiences
  2. Self-reporting: Documenting observations and insights
  3. Audio/Video recording: Objective review of classroom interactions
  4. Action research: Systematic inquiry into teaching practices

3. Teachers' Beliefs

What are teachers' beliefs and why are they important?

Definition (Raymond, 1997): Personal judgments formed from experiences. Teachers' beliefs are subjective, experience-based constructs that shape their teaching practices more powerfully than formal knowledge.

Why difficult to define/evaluate:

  1. Culturally bound: Shaped by cultural, educational, and societal norms
  2. Formed early: Stem from personal schooling experiences
  3. Resistant to change: Developed over many years through various experiences

Teachers' Beliefs About Learners

Belief View of Learner Teaching Approach
Resisters Resistant to learning Force/punishment to overcome resistance
Receptacles Passive containers Direct transmission of information
Raw Materials To be molded Shaping according to predetermined ideas
Partners Collaborators Joint discovery and collaboration
Individual Explorers Active discoverers Facilitation rather than instruction

Constraints on Effective Teaching

  • Diversity of learning styles (VARK)
  • Varied ability levels
  • Insufficient teaching materials
  • Overwhelming workloads
  • Lack of professional development
  • Teachers' own beliefs undermining effectiveness

4. Classroom Discourse & Teacher Talk

TTT vs. LTT (Teacher Talk Time vs. Learner Talk Time)

Teacher Talk Time (TTT) Learner Talk Time (LTT)
Modeling, explaining, managing Practicing, negotiating, expressing ideas
Essential for instruction Essential for language acquisition
Should be strategic and purposeful Should be maximized meaningfully

Pedagogic vs. Natural Discourse (Kramsch, 1998)

Pedagogic Discourse Natural Discourse
Formal, structured, teacher-led Informal, spontaneous, collaborative
Teacher as knowledge source Meaning co-constructed with learners
Accuracy & curriculum-focused Fluency & authentic communication
Known-answer (display) questions Learners initiate and express opinions

What are display vs. referential questions?

Display Questions: Teacher knows the answer (e.g., "What is the capital of France?"). Focuses on accuracy check, common in pedagogic discourse.

Referential Questions: Teacher does NOT know the answer (e.g., "What did you do last weekend?"). Focuses on genuine communication, common in natural discourse.

Effective teachers use more referential questions to promote authentic communication.

Classroom Interactional Competence (Walsh, 2011)

The ability to use interaction to mediate and assist learning through:

  • Appropriate questioning techniques
  • Effective turn-taking management
  • Constructive feedback provision
  • Clarification requests

5. Classroom Management

Misconceptions About Classroom Management

Misconception Reality
Only about misbehavior Preventing problems through positive engagement
Calmness means control Structure and understanding are key
Rewards/punishments are enough Teaching motivation and self-discipline is essential
Good teaching = good management Both planning and relationship-building needed

Four Key Components of Classroom Management

Physical Environment

Well-organized, welcoming space that reduces distractions

Management of Learning

Planning for diverse learning styles (VARK) and engagement

Procedures & Rules

Clear expectations and routines for security

Discipline Management

Guiding rather than punishing; restorative approaches

Types of Learning in Classroom Management (Evertson & Weinstein, 2006)

Academic Learning

Developing thinking skills, subject knowledge, and learning habits

Social Learning

Focusing on teamwork, empathy, respect, and collaboration

6. Teaching-Learning Process Dynamics

The Four Agents of Learning

Learner

Unique with prior knowledge, affect, and learning strategies

Teacher

Decides how learning occurs; pivotal factor in the process

Classroom Environment

Physical, social, psychological factors affecting learning

Curriculum

Structured framework guiding learning at all levels

Curriculum Definitions

William Kerr (1968)

"Curriculum is the totality of student experiences that occur in the educational process."

Emphasizes the comprehensive nature of educational experiences.

Lawrence Stenhouse (1975)

"Curriculum is a means by which teachers can formulate and test hypotheses about teaching and learning."

Views curriculum as a process open to adaptation and interpretation.

Types of Knowledge

Declarative Knowledge ("What")

Knowing facts or specific details

Example: "Paris is the capital of France."

Procedural Knowledge ("How")

Knowing how to perform a task

Example: Knowing how to write an essay or solve a math problem.

Motivation and Affect in Learning

According to Gardner (1995), motivation and attitude are central to learning a second language (L2). Affect encompasses motivation, desire, and positive attitude toward learning, significantly influencing learner engagement and persistence.

Learning Strategies

Metacognitive

Planning, monitoring, evaluating

Cognitive

Processing and remembering info

Social

Collaboration and interaction

Affective

Managing emotions and motivation

VARK Learning Preferences (Fleming & Mills, 1992)

Visual Auditory Reading/Writing Kinesthetic

Recognizing these preferences helps teachers adapt instruction for better outcomes.

Essay Builder: 20 Key Paragraphs

Below are 20 comprehensive paragraphs covering key concepts from all lectures. Use these to build essays, understand connections between topics, and prepare for exam questions.

Integrated Essay Paragraphs

1. The Evolution of Language Teaching Methodologies: The search for the perfect language teaching method has been a central concern in EFL history, progressing from Grammar-Translation's focus on accuracy and literary texts to Audio-Lingual's behaviorist drills, and finally to Communicative Language Teaching's emphasis on meaningful interaction. However, as research indicates, no single method can address the complex realities of diverse classrooms, leading to the contemporary understanding that effective teaching requires an eclectic, context-sensitive approach rather than rigid adherence to any one methodology.

2. The Paradigm Shift from Teaching to Learning: Modern language education has undergone a fundamental transformation from teacher-centered instruction to learner-centered approaches. This shift, influenced by constructivist and communicative theories, recognizes that learning is most effective when students actively participate in constructing knowledge rather than passively receiving it. The traditional "sage on the stage" model has given way to the teacher as "guide on the side," emphasizing facilitation over transmission and engagement over compliance.

3. Self-Monitoring as Professional Development: Self-monitoring represents a systematic approach to reflective practice that enables teachers to bridge the gap between their perceived teaching performance and classroom reality. By employing techniques such as lesson recording, journaling, and action research, teachers can move beyond the limitations of their "blind self" (aspects of their teaching visible to others but not themselves) and develop greater awareness and control over their instructional practices, ultimately leading to more intentional and effective teaching.

4. Teachers' Beliefs and Their Impact: Teachers' beliefs, defined as subjective judgments formed from personal and cultural experiences, exert a powerful influence on classroom practices often surpassing the impact of formal training. These deeply held assumptions about learnerswhether viewing them as resisters, receptacles, raw materials, partners, or individual explorersfundamentally shape instructional decisions, assessment approaches, and classroom relationships, highlighting why belief examination is crucial for effective teaching.

5. Classroom Discourse as a Learning Tool: Classroom discourse serves not merely as a medium of instruction but as a primary tool for language acquisition and cognitive development. The balance between Teacher Talk Time (TTT) and Learner Talk Time (LTT), the shift from display to referential questions, and the integration of pedagogic with natural discourse all contribute to creating communicative spaces where language is not only learned but authentically used, reflecting Walsh's concept of Classroom Interactional Competence.

6. Comprehensive Classroom Management: Effective classroom management transcends mere discipline to encompass the creation of an ecosystem conducive to both academic and social learning. This involves the strategic management of physical space, learning processes, classroom procedures, and behavioral guidanceall grounded in positive relationships and clear expectations rather than punitive measures, aligning with Evertson and Weinstein's dual focus on cognitive and social development.

7. The Teaching-Learning Ecosystem: Learning occurs within a dynamic ecosystem where four agentsthe unique learner, the adaptive teacher, the structured curriculum, and the supportive environmentinteract continuously. This perspective, emphasizing that students often learn more outside than inside classrooms, challenges traditional boundaries and recognizes learning as a lifelong, multidimensional process beginning before formal education and continuing throughout life.

8. Knowledge Types and Problem-Solving: Effective language learning requires both declarative knowledge (knowing "what"facts and information) and procedural knowledge (knowing "how"skills and processes). This dual knowledge base enables learners to move beyond rote memorization to genuine problem-solving, applying language strategically in authentic communicative situations rather than merely reproducing learned patterns.

9. Motivation and Affect in L2 Acquisition: Gardner's emphasis on motivation as central to second language learning highlights the crucial role of affectthe emotional and attitudinal dimensions of learning. Beyond cognitive strategies, learners' desires, confidence levels, and perceived relevance of the language significantly influence engagement, persistence, and ultimate success, necessitating pedagogical approaches that address these affective factors.

10. Learning Strategies and Styles: Effective learners employ metacognitive, cognitive, social, and affective strategies strategically, while teachers must recognize and accommodate diverse learning preferences through the VARK model (Visual, Auditory, Reading/Writing, Kinesthetic). This dual attention to how learners learn best and how they can improve their learning processes represents a key dimension of learner-centered instruction.

11. Curriculum as Process and Product: The contrast between Kerr's definition of curriculum as "the totality of student experiences" and Stenhouse's view of it as "a means to formulate and test hypotheses" captures the essential tension between curriculum as predetermined content and curriculum as dynamic process. Effective language teaching navigates this tension by providing structure while remaining responsive to emerging learner needs and contexts.

12. Teacher Roles in Modern Education: Contemporary teachers fulfill multifaceted roles as facilitators, planners, instructors, assessors, and mentorsa significant expansion beyond traditional knowledge-transmitter functions. This role diversification reflects the complexity of modern teaching and the recognition that effective instruction requires not only subject expertise but also skills in guidance, assessment, relationship-building, and adaptive planning.

13. Factors Undermining Teaching Effectiveness: Despite teachers' best intentions, effectiveness can be undermined by contextual constraints including diverse learning styles, varied ability levels, resource limitations, overwhelming workloads, and inadequate professional development. Additionally, teachers' own unexamined beliefs about learning and learners can inadvertently perpetuate ineffective practices, highlighting the need for systemic support coupled with reflective practice.

14. The Johari Window of Teacher Awareness: The Johari Window model applied to teachingdistinguishing open, secret, blind, and hidden aspects of teaching practiceprovides a powerful framework for understanding why self-monitoring is essential. By systematically reducing the "blind self" (teaching behaviors visible to others but not the teacher), educators can align their intentions with their actual practice, closing the gap between perceived and actual teaching effectiveness.

15. Pedagogic vs. Natural Discourse Balance: Kramsch's distinction between pedagogic discourse (structured, accuracy-focused, teacher-controlled) and natural discourse (spontaneous, fluency-focused, collaboratively constructed) highlights a key instructional challenge. Effective teaching strategically blends both, using pedagogic discourse to build foundational knowledge while creating spaces for natural discourse where language is used authentically for genuine communication.

16. Classroom Management Misconceptions: Common misconceptions about classroom managementthat it's primarily about discipline, that calmness equals control, that rewards/punishments suffice, or that good teaching automatically ensures good managementoften lead to ineffective practices. In reality, management is a proactive, multifaceted skill involving environmental design, relationship-building, procedural clarity, and instructional engagement.

17. The Learner as Central Agent: Each learner enters the classroom with a unique combination of prior knowledge, affective disposition, and learning strategieswhat might be called their "learning history." Effective teaching recognizes this uniqueness, adapting instruction to accommodate different starting points, motivations, and approaches rather than expecting uniform responses to standardized instruction.

18. Display vs. Referential Questions: The distinction between display questions (to which teachers know answers) and referential questions (to which they don't) has profound implications for classroom interaction. While display questions check comprehension and recall, referential questions promote authentic communication, longer learner responses, and genuine meaning negotiationkey components of language acquisition beyond the structural level.

19. Methods as Historical Artifacts: Each major teaching methodfrom Grammar-Translation to Suggestopediaemerged from specific historical contexts and theoretical assumptions about language and learning. Understanding these methods not as prescriptions but as historical artifacts helps teachers critically evaluate their underlying principles and selectively integrate useful techniques while avoiding rigid adherence to any single approach.

20. Holistic Educational Success: Ultimately, successful language education requires the harmonious integration of effective teaching practices, reflective teacher development, examined beliefs, strategic discourse management, supportive classroom environments, and responsive curricula. This holistic approach recognizes that language learning is not merely cognitive but also social, emotional, and culturala complex process best supported by equally complex and adaptive teaching.
