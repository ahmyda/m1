Lecture 1: Introducing Applied Linguistics

Defining Applied Linguistics

Key Definitions:

  • Kaplan and Widdowson (1992): "A mediation between theory and practice."
  • Brumfit (1977): "The theoretical and empirical investigation of real world problems in which language is a central issue."
  • Spolsky (2005): Focuses on resolving language-based problems encountered in the real world.
  • AAAL: "An interdisciplinary field of inquiry that addresses a broad range of language-related issues."

What is the difference between Applied Linguistics and Linguistics Applied?

Linguistics Applied: Assumes language problems can be solved by direct application of linguistic concepts. Language problems = linguistic solutions.

Applied Linguistics: Recognizes that linguistic insights require interpretation and mediation. It's a multilateral process that relates linguistic perspectives with other perceptions of reality (psychology, sociology, education, etc.).

Scope of Applied Linguistics

Language & Education

  • First-language education
  • Additional-language education
  • Clinical linguistics
  • Language testing

Language, Work & Law

  • Workplace communication
  • Language planning
  • Forensic linguistics

Language, Information & Effect

  • Literary stylistics
  • Critical Discourse Analysis
  • Translation & interpretation
  • Lexicography

Real-World Questions Addressed by AL

  • Should children speaking a dialect be encouraged to maintain it or steered towards the standard form?
  • In multilingual communities, which languages should be used in schools?
  • Should deaf children learn sign language or lip reading/speaking?
  • Should the growth of English as an international lingua franca be welcomed or deplored?
  • Is language being used for political oppression and indoctrination?

Lecture 2: Contrastive Analysis

Historical Context

Developed in the 1950s-1960s, based on behaviorist psychology (habit formation theory). Lado (1957) provided the first comprehensive theoretical treatment.

What are the four assumptions of Contrastive Analysis according to Lee (1968)?

  1. The prime cause of difficulty in L2 learning is interference from L1
  2. The greater the differences between languages, the more acute the learning difficulties
  3. Comparison between languages predicts difficulties and errors
  4. What to teach = sum of differences established by CA (subtract similarities)

Theory of Transfer

Positive Transfer

Facilitates L2 learning when L1 and L2 structures are similar

Example: Similar SVO word order in English and French

Negative Transfer (Interference)

Impedes L2 learning when L1 and L2 structures differ

Example: Arabic speakers omitting verb 'be' in English (*"He student")

Hierarchy of Difficulty (Stockwell et al., 1965)

Type Description Example (Arabic → English) Difficulty Level
Split L1 has one form, L2 has two or more Arabic has /b/, English has /p/ and /b/ Greatest difficulty
New Category Exists in L2 but not in L1 Indefinite articles (a/an) in English High difficulty
Absent Category Exists in L1 but absent in L2 Grammatical gender in Arabic Moderate difficulty
Coalesced Forms L1 has multiple forms, L2 has one Multiple Arabic negators vs. English "not" Lower difficulty
Complete Correspondence Same or similar forms in both languages Simple Past Tense in both Least difficulty

CA Criticism

  • Based on behaviorist "habit-formation" neglecting cognitive processes
  • Overprediction of errors (predicted difficulties don't always occur)
  • Underprediction of errors (fails to predict errors like *"goed")
  • Requires perfect knowledge of both languages being contrasted
  • Focuses only on L1 interference, ignoring other error sources

Lecture 3: Error Analysis

Errors vs. Mistakes (Brown, 1993)

Mistakes

  • Performance errors (slips)
  • Random and non-systematic
  • Can be self-corrected
  • Due to fatigue, excitement, etc.
  • Not reflective of competence

Errors

  • Systematic deviations
  • Reflect learner's competence
  • Cannot be self-corrected
  • Product of current L2 development stage
  • Show rule-governed behavior

EA Methodology (8 Steps)

  1. Collection of errors from learner productions
  2. Identification of errors against L2 norms
  3. Classification of error types (see below)
  4. Statement of relative frequency of error types
  5. Identification of areas of difficulty in L2
  6. Determination of error sources (L1 transfer, overgeneralization, etc.)
  7. Determination of error seriousness in communication
  8. Remedy through teaching (drills, activities, etc.)

Classification of Errors

Errors of Competence

Application of L2 rules that don't correspond to L2 norms. Take time to correct.

Errors of Performance (Mistakes)

Recognized by speakers and corrected easily.

Interlingual (Transfer) Errors

Due to L1 influence

  • Phonological: /lbl/ for /lpl/
  • Morphological: "book Ahmad" for "Ahmad's book"
  • Syntactic: "Huda student" for "Huda is a student"
  • Semantic: "close the TV" for "turn off the TV"
Intralingual (Overgeneralization) Errors

Due to L2 system influence

  • Phonological: pronouncing "count" as /kAnt/
  • Morphological: "comed" for "came"
  • Semantic: literal translation of idioms
  • Syntactic: "He is comes" for "He is coming"

EA vs. CA Comparison

Contrastive Analysis (CA) Error Analysis (EA)
Starts with language comparison Starts with learner errors
Predicts areas of difficulty Analyzes actual attested problems
Focuses on L1 interference only Considers multiple error sources
Theoretical problems (equivalence) More practical, pedagogical focus
Teacher-centered approach Learner-centered approach

EA Criticism

  • Classification issues: Same error can be classified differently
  • Progression insights: Only shows what learner can't do at one time point (cross-sectional)
  • Error identification difficulty: Depends on L2 norms which vary by context
  • Avoidance strategy: Learners may avoid structures where they'd make errors

Lecture 4: Interlanguage

Key Concepts

Interlanguage (IL): The intermediate linguistic system between L1 and L2, created by learners during SLA.

Alternative terms: Approximative systems (Nemser, 1971), Transitional competence (Corder, 1967).

What are Selinker's five central cognitive processes in IL formation?

  1. L1 transfer: Influence of native language
  2. Transfer of training: From teachers' instruction methods
  3. Strategies of L2 learning: Approaches to learning language elements
  4. Strategies of L2 communication: Ways of communicating with native speakers
  5. Overgeneralization of L2 rules: Extending rules beyond acceptable use

IL Assumptions

  1. Learners construct their own linguistic system (different from both L1 and L2)
  2. System is based on L2 input received
  3. Learners rely on this system at successive learning stages
  4. System rarely becomes identical to L2 norm

Characteristics of Interlanguage

Systematic

Governed by rules (rule-governed behavior)

Internally Consistent

Linguistic system in its own right

Reduced Systems

Fewer and simpler rules than target language

Permeable

Rules are incomplete and in flux

Fossilization

Permanent stabilization of errors despite exposure and instruction.

Causes (Selinker & Lamendella, 1978):

  • Low motivation (psychological/social reasons)
  • Age (older learners retain foreign accent)
  • Limited L2 input (quality and quantity)

IL Methodology

Selinker (1972) identified essential components:

  1. L1 utterances produced by learner
  2. IL utterances produced by learner
  3. L2 utterances used by native speakers

Note: IL methodology incorporates both CA (contrasts L1 and L2) and EA (contrasts learner performance and L2).

IL Criticism

  • No concrete assertions on describing changing linguistic systems
  • Requires large body of data (longitudinal studies needed)
  • Difficult to observe truly systematic form due to multiple variables

Lecture 5: Language Learning Strategies

Defining Learning Strategies

Oxford (1990): "Tools for active, self-directed involvement, essential for developing communicative competence."

Cohen (1998): Includes both L2 learning strategies and L2 use strategies.

What are the main characteristics of language learning strategies?

  1. Involve both linguistic and non-linguistic behavior
  2. Can be behavioral (observable) or mental (unobservable)
  3. Amenable to change (can be modified, rejected, learned)
  4. Systematic (employed based on knowledge of problem)
  5. Contribute indirectly (and sometimes directly) to learning
  6. Vary based on task type and individual preferences

O'Malley et al.'s Framework

Metacognitive Strategies

Executive skills: planning, monitoring, evaluating

Examples: Selective attention, self-monitoring, functional planning

Cognitive Strategies

Direct manipulation of learning materials

Examples: Repetition, resourcing, translation, note-taking

Social Strategies

Learning through interaction with others

Examples: Cooperation, questioning for clarification

Oxford's Classification (1990)

Direct Strategies

Directly involved in L2, require mental processing of language

  • Memory strategies: Remembering/retrieving information
  • Cognitive strategies: Understanding/producing language
  • Compensation strategies: Making up for lack of knowledge

Indirect Strategies

Provide indirect support for language learning

  • Metacognitive strategies: Coordinating learning process
  • Affective strategies: Regulating emotions
  • Social strategies: Learning with others

Factors Affecting Strategy Choice

Factor Impact on Strategy Use
Age Young children: task-specific, simple strategies. Older learners: generalized, complex strategies
Aptitude Weak relationship; possibly related to general intelligence factor
Motivation Highly motivated learners use more strategies; integrative motivation → communication-oriented strategies
Personality Extroverts use more meaning-search strategies; introverts use different approaches
Background More years of study → more functional practice strategies; expert vs. novice differences
Situational Factors Task type influences strategy choice (vocabulary tasks → resourcing; listening tasks → note-taking)

Strategy Training in Teaching

Key principles (Cook, 2001):

  1. Make students aware of good language learning strategies
  2. Train students to develop learning independence
  3. Make students aware of a range of strategy options
  4. Explain similarities/differences between L2 learning and other subjects

Goal: Help learners become independent and take responsibility for their learning.

Essay Builder: Applied Linguistics

Below are 20 comprehensive paragraphs covering key concepts from all five lectures. Use these to build essays, understand connections between topics, and prepare for exam questions.

Integrated Essay Paragraphs

1. The Nature and Scope of Applied Linguistics: Applied Linguistics represents an interdisciplinary field that serves as a crucial bridge between linguistic theory and real-world language problems. Unlike theoretical linguistics which examines language as an abstract system, applied linguistics focuses on practical issues encountered by diverse stakeholders including learners, teachers, translators, policymakers, and professionals across various domains. As defined by Brumfit (1977), it involves "the theoretical and empirical investigation of real world problems in which language is a central issue," emphasizing its problem-solving orientation and practical relevance in educational, social, and professional contexts.

2. Contrastive Analysis: Foundations and Limitations: Developed during the 1950s-1960s under the influence of behaviorist psychology, Contrastive Analysis (CA) represented the first systematic attempt to predict second language learning difficulties through comparison of linguistic systems. Based on Lado's (1957) premise that "we can predict and describe the patterns that will cause difficulty in learning by comparing systematically the language and culture to be learned with the native language," CA established hierarchies of difficulty ranging from splits (where L1 has one form, L2 has multiple) to complete correspondence. However, its behaviorist foundations and overreliance on L1 interference as the sole source of difficulty ultimately limited its predictive accuracy and theoretical validity.

3. Error Analysis: Shifting Focus to the Learner: Emerging in the 1970s as a reaction to CA's limitations, Error Analysis (EA) marked a significant paradigm shift from teacher-centered to learner-centered approaches in applied linguistics. Rather than predicting difficulties through language comparison, EA focused on analyzing actual errors produced by learners, recognizing them as systematic indicators of underlying competence rather than random mistakes. This approach distinguished between errors (systematic deviations reflecting current competence) and mistakes (performance slips), and classified errors as either interlingual (L1 transfer) or intralingual (L2 overgeneralization), providing valuable insights into learners' developing linguistic systems.

4. The Interlanguage Hypothesis: Building on EA's insights, the Interlanguage (IL) hypothesis proposed by Selinker (1972) conceptualized learners' language as a dynamic, rule-governed system existing independently between L1 and L2. This "approximative system" develops through five central cognitive processes: L1 transfer, transfer of training, learning strategies, communication strategies, and overgeneralization. IL is characterized by systematicity, internal consistency, permeability, and susceptibility to fossilization—the permanent stabilization of non-target forms. This theoretical framework recognized learners as active constructors of linguistic knowledge rather than passive recipients of language input.

5. Language Learning Strategies: Empowering Learners: The study of Language Learning Strategies represents a natural progression toward understanding how learners actively engage with the language learning process. Oxford's (1990) classification distinguishes between direct strategies (memory, cognitive, compensation) that involve mental processing of language, and indirect strategies (metacognitive, affective, social) that support the learning process. Research demonstrates that effective strategy use correlates with higher proficiency and greater learner autonomy, and that strategies can be systematically taught and developed, making this area particularly relevant for pedagogical applications and learner training programs.

6. From CA to IL: Theoretical Evolution in SLA: The theoretical evolution from Contrastive Analysis through Error Analysis to Interlanguage theory reflects broader shifts in psychology from behaviorist to cognitive paradigms. While CA focused exclusively on external linguistic comparison and L1 interference, EA introduced the analysis of learner data, and IL theory conceptualized the learner's internal linguistic system. This progression demonstrates increasing recognition of learners' active cognitive role in constructing linguistic knowledge, moving from a deficit model (focusing on errors and difficulties) to a developmental model (understanding systematic progression toward target language norms).

7. Methodological Contributions and Limitations: Each approach in applied linguistics has contributed distinct methodological tools while facing specific limitations. CA introduced systematic language comparison but suffered from overprediction and theoretical rigidity. EA developed error classification systems but faced challenges in distinguishing error types and accounting for avoidance strategies. IL theory emphasized longitudinal study but required extensive data collection. Learning strategy research employed various assessment methods but confronted issues of self-report reliability. Understanding these methodological strengths and limitations is crucial for critically evaluating research in applied linguistics.

8. Pedagogical Implications of Applied Linguistics: Applied linguistics research has profoundly influenced language teaching methodologies and materials development. CA informed the creation of contrastive teaching materials targeting predicted difficulties. EA led to error-focused remediation activities and more tolerant attitudes toward learner errors. IL theory encouraged teachers to view learner language as a legitimate developing system rather than defective target language. Strategy training has been incorporated into curricula to develop learner autonomy. These applications demonstrate how theoretical insights translate into practical classroom interventions.

9. Individual Differences in Language Learning: Research on language learning strategies has highlighted the significant role of individual learner variables including age, aptitude, motivation, personality, and learning background. Oxford's (1990) findings that highly motivated learners use more comprehensive strategy repertoires, and that extroverts employ different communication strategies than introverts, underscore the importance of considering learner individuality in both research and teaching. These factors interact complexly with situational variables such as task type and learning context, creating diverse learning profiles that resist simple categorization.

10. Fossilization: Theoretical Explanations and Practical Implications: The phenomenon of fossilization, where certain non-target features become permanent in a learner's interlanguage despite continued exposure and instruction, represents a significant challenge in second language acquisition. Selinker and Lamendella's (1978) identification of contributing factors—including low motivation, age-related constraints, and limited input quality—has important implications for teaching. Understanding fossilization helps explain why some errors persist despite correction and suggests that pedagogical efforts might be more productively directed toward communicative effectiveness rather than complete accuracy for certain learner populations.

11. Applied Linguistics as Interdisciplinary Mediation: The distinction between "Applied Linguistics" and "Linguistics Applied" highlighted by Widdowson and others emphasizes that applied linguistics involves mediation rather than direct application. This means linguistic insights must be interpreted and integrated with knowledge from psychology, sociology, education, and other relevant fields to address real-world language problems. This interdisciplinary nature allows applied linguistics to address complex issues like language planning, forensic linguistics, and clinical language disorders that require multifaceted approaches beyond purely linguistic analysis.

12. Error Classification Systems: Error Analysis developed sophisticated classification systems distinguishing between errors of competence (reflecting underlying knowledge gaps) and errors of performance (temporary slips), and between interlingual errors (attributable to L1 transfer) and intralingual errors (resulting from L2 system overgeneralization). These classifications help teachers and researchers identify error sources and develop appropriate interventions. For example, interlingual phonological errors might require contrastive pronunciation practice, while intralingual morphological errors might benefit from clarification of rule boundaries and exceptions.

13. Strategy Training in Language Education: The pedagogical application of learning strategy research, as advocated by Cook (2001) and Oxford (1990), involves making learners aware of strategic options, helping them develop independence, and teaching transferable learning skills. Effective strategy training goes beyond teaching specific techniques to developing metacognitive awareness—helping learners understand how they learn best and how to adapt strategies to different tasks and contexts. This approach aligns with broader educational goals of developing autonomous, lifelong learners capable of directing their own learning processes.

14. The Hierarchy of Difficulty in CA: Stockwell, Bowen, and Martin's (1965) hierarchy of difficulty, ranging from splits (most difficult) through new categories, absent categories, and coalesced forms to complete correspondence (least difficult), provided a systematic framework for predicting learning challenges. This hierarchy recognized that not all differences between languages pose equal difficulties, and that certain types of contrasts (like having to distinguish where the L1 doesn't) create greater learning burdens than others. While later research questioned the absolute predictive power of this hierarchy, it represented an important step toward systematic difficulty prediction.

15. Social and Affective Dimensions in Strategy Use: Oxford's classification highlights the importance of social and affective strategies alongside cognitive and metacognitive ones. Affective strategies for managing emotions (like anxiety reduction techniques) and social strategies for learning through interaction (like cooperation and questioning) acknowledge that language learning involves more than cognitive processing. These dimensions connect strategy research with broader concerns in language education regarding learner anxiety, motivation, and classroom climate, demonstrating the holistic nature of effective language learning.

16. IL as a Reduced but Systematic System: Interlanguage theory posits that learners develop linguistic systems that are reduced in complexity compared to the target language but nevertheless systematic and rule-governed. These systems exhibit characteristics of natural languages (like internal consistency and constraint by linguistic universals) while also showing unique features resulting from the learning process itself (like permeability and fossilization). This view legitimizes learner language as a valid object of linguistic study while explaining its distinctive properties.

17. Real-World Applications Beyond Language Teaching: While language teaching has historically been a major focus, applied linguistics addresses diverse real-world problems including language policy planning, forensic text analysis, clinical assessment of language disorders, workplace communication training, and dictionary compilation. These applications demonstrate the field's relevance across multiple domains and its capacity to draw on linguistic theory to address practical concerns in law, medicine, business, and social policy.

18. From Error Correction to Strategic Competence: The evolution from CA's focus on preventing errors through contrastive teaching to strategy research's emphasis on developing strategic competence reflects a broader shift in language education goals. Rather than merely avoiding errors, contemporary approaches aim to develop learners' ability to use communication strategies effectively, manage their own learning processes, and employ compensation strategies when facing linguistic limitations. This represents a more realistic and communicative orientation aligned with how language is actually used outside classroom contexts.

19. Research Methodologies Across Approaches: Different approaches in applied linguistics employ distinct research methodologies: CA relies on systematic language comparison, EA on error collection and classification, IL theory on longitudinal observation of learner language development, and strategy research often on questionnaires, interviews, and think-aloud protocols. Each methodology offers particular insights while presenting specific challenges regarding data collection, analysis, and interpretation, requiring researchers to match methodological choices carefully to research questions.

20. Integration and Future Directions: Contemporary applied linguistics increasingly integrates insights from multiple approaches, recognizing that language learning involves complex interactions between linguistic systems, cognitive processes, individual differences, and social contexts. Future directions likely involve greater integration of neuroscientific findings, more sophisticated computational modeling of language development, and increased attention to multilingual rather than bilingual contexts. This integrative, multidisciplinary approach continues to advance our understanding of language-related problems and their solutions.
